# CHA 11.11

Let $B$ be a denumerable set and let $A$ be a nonempty set of unspecified cardinality. If $f : A \to B$ is a one-to-one function, then what can be said about the cardinality of $A$? Explain

---

## Solution

To determine the cardinality of $A$, we use the fact that $f$ is injective to construct a bijection. We restrict the codomain of $f$ to its image $f(A)$, a subset of $B$. Define the function
$$g := f\big|_{f(A)} : A \to f(A)
$$
be the restriction of $f$ to its image. Since $f$ is injective, $g$ is injective (the proof is straightforward). Moreover, by definition of $f(A)$, for all $y \in f(A)$, there exists $x \in A$ such that $f(x) = y$. Thus, $g$ is surjective. Thus, $g$ is a bijection, which means $A$ and $f(A)$ have the same cardinality.

We consider two cases:

**Case 1: $f(A)$ is finite.** Since $g : A \to f(A)$ is a bijection and $f(A)$ is finite, it follows from Result 2-c that $A$ and $f(A)$ have the same cardinality. Therefore, $A$ is finite.

**Case 2: $f(A)$ is infinite.** Since $B$ is denumerable and $f(A) \subseteq B$, it follows from Theorem 11.04 that $f(A)$ is also denumerable. Since $f(A)$ is denumerable, there exists a bijection $h : \mathbb{N} \to f(A)$. Since $g : A \to f(A)$ is a bijection, it follows from Theorem 10.15 that $g$ has an inverse $g^{-1} : f(A) \to A$, which is a well-defined function and bijective. By Corollary 10.12, the composition $g^{-1} \circ h : \mathbb{N} \to A$ is a bijection. Therefore, $A$ is denumerable.

---

Here, we say that **$A$ is countable**.

---

**Remark:** The key technique in this solution is **codomain restriction**. Since $f$ is injective but we don't know if it's surjective onto $B$, we cannot use $f$ directly to establish a bijection. By restricting the codomain to its image $f(A)$, we "shrink" the target set to exactly the values that $f$ reaches. This transforms an injection into a bijection, which allows us to compare cardinalities. This technique is fundamental in arguments involving cardinality. $\blacksquare$
