# CHA 11.09

Show that every denumerable set $A$ can be partitioned into two denumerable subsets of $A$.

---

## Proof

Let $A$ be a denumerable set; that is, $A = \{a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots\}$ where $a_i \in A$ for all $i\in\mathbb{N}$. We choose the subsets $B$ and $C$ defined as
$$
\begin{align*}
B &:= \{a_1, a_3, a_5, \dots\} = \{a_{2k-1} : k\in\mathbb{N}\}, \\
C &:= \{a_2, a_4, a_6, \dots\} = \{a_{2k} : k\in\mathbb{N}\}.
\end{align*}
$$

Since $B$ and $C$ are subsets of $A$, we have $B\cup C \subseteq A$. Also, since every element of $A$ is either in $B$ or in $C$, we have $A \subseteq B\cup C$. Thus, $A = B\cup C$. Moreover, since $B$ and $C$ are disjoint, we have $B\cap C = \emptyset$. Therefore, $\{B,C\}$ is a partition of $A$.

Next, we show that $B$ and $C$ are denumerable. Define $f:\mathbb{N}\to B$ by $f(k) := a_{2k-1}$ for all $k\in\mathbb{N}$.

(One-to-one) Assume $f(k_1) = f(k_2)$ for some $k_1, k_2 \in \mathbb{N}$. Then $a_{2k_1-1} = a_{2k_2-1}$. Since the enumeration $\{a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots\}$ has distinct elements (each index corresponds to exactly one element), we have $2k_1 - 1 = 2k_2 - 1$. Therefore, $k_1 = k_2$, and $f$ is one-to-one.

(Onto) Let $b\in B$. By definition of $B$, we can find $k\in\mathbb{N}$ such that $b = a_{2k-1}$. Then $f(k) = a_{2k-1} = b$, so $b$ is in the range of $f$. Thus, $f$ is onto.

Therefore, $f$ is a bijection, and so $B$ is denumerable. Similarly, define $g:\mathbb{N}\to C$ by $g(k) := a_{2k}$ for all $k\in\mathbb{N}$. By the same reasoning, $g$ is a bijection. Therefore, $C$ is denumerable. $\blacksquare$
